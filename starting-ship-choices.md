# How You Got the Ship

Three offers are on the table. Pick one. None of them are clean.

---

## Option 1: The Patron's Ship

A faction or megacorp owns the ship. You fly it. They don't ask for rent and they don't ask for a cut. What they ask for is harder to price.

**What you get.** A ship, and the long shadow of an organization standing behind it. Maintenance accounts at every port your patron has a presence in, which is a lot of ports. A standing line of credit for fuel and minor repairs. When you call port control, they recognize the transponder and they have a slot for you. When the drive coil fails at three in the morning, there is somebody to call and they answer.

The patron's reputation precedes you in the good ways too. Doors open. Customs officials wave you through. The right contact in the right port returns your messages on the same day.

**What it costs.** The ship wears your patron's colors. Their transponder ID. Their livery on the hull. You cannot dock anonymously and you cannot do anything anonymously. Wherever you go, that is who walks in the door before you do. Their friends expect favors and assume your help. Their enemies assume your hostility before you have done anything to earn it. Ports that hate your patron will not let you dock at all, and a surprising number of ports in this sector hate at least one of the powers operating in it.

You also carry a liaison. Officially they are aboard to "support operations." In practice, they are the patron's eyes, ears, and conscience. They are not a soldier and they are not in command. They file regular reports you do not see. They have routines, opinions, and a personality you did not pick. You cannot remove them without ending the arrangement, and ending the arrangement means losing the ship.

The patron has not yet asked anything specific of you. They will.

**The open question.** What does the patron actually want from you, and how long until you find out?

---

## Option 2: The Dead Captain's Ship

The ship's previous owner died. You were there. There was no time for a clean handoff and the paperwork is what you could put together after the fact. Some ports will not look too closely if you don't give them a reason to. Other ports will.

**What you get.** A ship and nobody telling you what to do with it. No patron, no charter, no liaison, no list. You answer to yourselves. That is the entire upside, and for some crews it is enough.

**What it costs.** Your title is thin. A relative, a creditor, a business partner, or a clerk with the right paperwork could contest it, and you would probably lose. You will avoid the ports where that is likely. That turns out to be more ports than you'd think.

You have no backers. No maintenance accounts. No standing credit at any port. When the drive coil fails at three in the morning, you are paying cash to whoever is awake.

Half the ship's systems are locked behind credentials you don't have. Logs you can't read, lockers you can't open, a nav computer that asks for a password before it remembers its own routes. You can crack some of it. The rest you will work around for a long time, and you will not always know what you are working around.

The previous owner had debts, enemies, and obligations you did not take on but inherited anyway. Their creditors will find you before you find them. People who were owed favors will call them in. People who were owed grudges will collect on them. Strangers in ports you have never been to will recognize the ship and have opinions about it before you say a word.

And whatever killed the previous owner happened for a reason. That reason has not gone away.

**The open question.** Whose problem are you actually inheriting, and how much of it can you survive?

---

## Option 3: The Licensed Ship

A faction has issued you a charter. The ship is yours, conditionally. The condition is that you use it against a specific list of their enemies: their secrets, their assets, their reputations. The license is mostly legal in the territory of the issuing faction. It is paper-thin everywhere else.

**What you get.** A ship, a charter document on official letterhead, and a list. The list names corporations, officials, and rival factions whose property and information you are licensed to take. Successful operations pay shares from whatever you bring in. Documents sell. Prototypes sell. Certain kinds of leverage sell very well. The charter opens some legal doors in your patron's territory, including immunity from a handful of charges that would otherwise stick.

Outside your patron's territory, the charter is a piece of paper that may or may not impress the people you show it to.

**What it costs.** The list is not a kill order. Your patron prefers things done quietly. Documents copied. People followed. Prototypes lifted. Ledgers leaked. Executives walked out a side door with a job offer they didn't expect. Loud work pays less and burns standing with the patron. Loud work that goes wrong gets disowned. If you are caught doing what you are licensed to do, the charter helps. If you are caught doing it badly, it doesn't.

The targets fight back. Not with fleets, usually. With lawyers. With investigators. With their own quiet operatives. With rumors that follow you from port to port and arrive before you do. People you have never met will know your faces and your reputations before you set foot on their station.

The list also changes. Names get added. Names get removed. You will not always be told why, and neither the addition nor the removal makes the person on it any less dangerous. People you used to be licensed to act against do not always feel forgiving once they are free to act against you.

**The open question.** What did the people on the list do to end up there, and what will the people who get crossed off do once they're free to act?

---

Pick the one your crew can live with. The downside you accept now is the engine of the campaign.
